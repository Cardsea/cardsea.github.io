SDR Legal Information & Regulations

⚠️ Legal Disclaimer

This information is for educational purposes only and should not be considered legal advice. Laws vary by jurisdiction and change over time. Always consult local regulations and legal professionals for specific guidance.

General Principles

  • Receiving is generally legal: In most countries, listening to radio transmissions is legal
  • Transmitting requires authorization: Broadcasting typically requires licenses
  • Encrypted/scrambled signals: Attempting to decrypt may be illegal
  • Privacy laws apply: Some communications are protected by privacy laws

United States Regulations

FCC Rules (47 CFR)

  • Part 15: Unlicensed devices and receivers
  • Part 97: Amateur radio service
  • Part 2.102: Authorized frequency bands

Key Points:

  • Receiving most radio signals is legal under 47 CFR 2.102
  • Exception: Cellular phone calls (illegal to intentionally intercept)
  • Transmitting requires appropriate license for frequency/power
  • Some frequencies are restricted (government, military)

Legal Frequency Ranges for Reception:

  • AM/FM Broadcast: 530-1700 kHz, 88-108 MHz
  • Amateur Radio: Various bands (see Part 97)
  • Aviation: 108-137 MHz (VHF), 960-1215 MHz (DME/TACAN)
  • Weather: 162.4-162.55 MHz
  • ISM Bands: 902-928 MHz, 2.4-2.485 GHz, others

European Union

  • Radio Equipment Directive (RED): 2014/53/EU
  • ECC Report 32: Short Range Device regulations
  • National variations: Each EU country may have additional rules
  • ETSI standards: Technical requirements for radio equipment

What You CAN Generally Do

  • ✅ Listen to amateur radio communications
  • ✅ Receive broadcast radio and TV
  • ✅ Monitor public service communications (police, fire - where not encrypted)
  • ✅ Receive satellite transmissions
  • ✅ Analyze signal characteristics for research
  • ✅ Use ISM bands with appropriate power limits
  • ✅ Transmit on amateur bands with proper license

What You CANNOT/SHOULD NOT Do

  • ❌ Intercept encrypted communications with intent to decrypt
  • ❌ Intentionally receive cellular phone calls
  • ❌ Retransmit copyrighted broadcast content
  • ❌ Cause harmful interference to licensed services
  • ❌ Transmit without proper authorization
  • ❌ Access communication systems you don't own without permission
  • ❌ Violate privacy laws regarding overheard communications

Best Practices for SDR Users

  • Know your local laws: Research regulations in your country/region
  • Get proper licenses: Obtain amateur radio license for transmission
  • Respect privacy: Don't share private communications you may overhear
  • Educational use: Focus on learning and experimentation
  • Test responsibly: Only test on systems you own or have permission to test
  • Document your activities: Keep records of your research and experiments

Cybersecurity & Research Ethics

  • Responsible disclosure: Report vulnerabilities to vendors appropriately
  • Authorized testing only: Don't test security on systems you don't own
  • Academic research: Follow institutional review board guidelines
  • Professional use: Adhere to industry ethics and legal frameworks

Remember

Technology moves fast, but laws can be slow to catch up. When in doubt, err on the side of caution and seek legal advice. The SDR community values responsible use and ethical behavior - please be a good representative of this technology.
