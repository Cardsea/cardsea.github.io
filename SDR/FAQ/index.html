What is Software Defined Radio (SDR)?

SDR is a radio communication system where components that have been traditionally implemented in hardware are instead implemented by means of software on a personal computer or embedded system.

What can I do with an SDR?

With an SDR, you can:

  • Listen to FM/AM radio broadcasts
  • Monitor air traffic control communications
  • Receive weather satellite images
  • Analyze wireless protocols
  • Study signal processing concepts
  • Experiment with digital modes

What SDR hardware should I start with?

For beginners, the RTL-SDR dongles are excellent starting points:

  • RTL-SDR v3: ~$35, great for receiving (24MHz - 1.7GHz)
  • Airspy Mini: ~$99, better performance and sensitivity
  • HackRF One: ~$300, can transmit and receive (1MHz - 6GHz)

What software do I need?

Popular SDR software includes:

  • SDR#: Windows-friendly, beginner-friendly interface
  • GQRX: Cross-platform, GNU Radio based
  • CubicSDR: Cross-platform with waterfall display
  • GNU Radio: Advanced signal processing framework

Do I need a ham radio license?

For receiving only (listening), no license is required in most countries. However, if you plan to transmit, you'll need appropriate licenses depending on the frequencies and power levels you want to use.

What frequencies can I listen to legally?

This varies by country, but generally you can receive:

  • Broadcast FM/AM radio
  • Weather services
  • Amateur radio bands
  • Aviation communications (non-scrambled)
  • ISM bands

Note: Always check local regulations - see our legal information page.

Why is my reception poor?

Common issues and solutions:

  • Antenna: Use appropriate antenna for your frequency range
  • Interference: Move away from computers, LED lights, USB3 ports
  • Gain settings: Adjust RF and IF gain in your software
  • Filters: Use bandpass filters for specific frequency ranges

Can SDR be used for cybersecurity research?

Yes! SDR is valuable for:

  • Analyzing wireless protocols and their security
  • Studying IoT device communications
  • Understanding radio frequency attacks
  • Testing wireless security implementations

Important: Only analyze systems you own or have explicit permission to test.
